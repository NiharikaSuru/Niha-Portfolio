Overview

Reimagining Fantasy Sports

F2MX (Fan 2 Market Experience) is a next-generation fantasy sports platform that bridges the gap between sports fandom and stock market-style trading. Users buy and sell shares in athletes, with values fluctuating based on real-world performance.

The challenge was to make complex financial concepts accessible to sports fans while providing the depth that serious traders demand – all within a mobile-first experience that could be used during live games.

The Challenge

Data Overload Kills Engagement

Fantasy sports platforms are notorious for overwhelming users with statistics. Through user research and competitive analysis, I identified critical pain points:

  • Information Overload: Screens packed with stats, charts, and numbers caused decision paralysis during time-sensitive trading
  • Complex Onboarding: New users struggled to understand trading mechanics, leading to high early drop-off rates
  • Real-Time Chaos: During live games, rapidly changing data made it nearly impossible to make informed decisions
  • One-Size-Fits-All: Casual fans and pro traders had vastly different needs, but interfaces didn't adapt
  • Mobile Afterthought: Most platforms were desktop-first, with cramped mobile experiences

Understanding how users consume sports data in real-time environments

Process

Design Approach

I took a mobile-first approach, designing for the most constrained environment first and scaling up from there.

1

User Segmentation

Identified three distinct user types: casual fans, active traders, and data-driven analysts – each with unique needs.

2

Information Hierarchy

Ruthlessly prioritized what data appears when, using progressive disclosure to reduce cognitive load.

3

Rapid Prototyping

Created 50+ iterations of key screens, testing during live games to validate real-world usability.

4

Performance Testing

Optimized for real-time updates without UI jank or battery drain during extended sessions.

Solution

Key Design Innovations

The solution centered on progressive disclosure, smart defaults, and contextual information delivery.

Progressive Disclosure

Critical info first, details on demand. Users see what they need when they need it.

Quick Trade

One-tap trading for decisive moments. No confirmation dialogs during live action.

Smart Alerts

AI-powered notifications for price movements, injury news, and trading opportunities.

Adaptive Interface

UI complexity adjusts based on user expertise level and current activity.

Mobile First

Designed for Speed

Every interaction was optimized for one-handed use and split-second decisions. Large touch targets, swipe gestures, and haptic feedback make trading feel instant and satisfying.

Portfolio overview and quick trade interfaces designed for live game scenarios

Design Decisions

Visual & Interaction Principles

The visual language needed to feel premium and trustworthy (like a financial app) while maintaining the excitement and energy of sports.

  • Dark Mode Default: Easier on eyes during extended viewing sessions and live game environments (bars, stadiums)
  • Color-Coded Trends: Instant visual feedback for price movements – green up, red down, no cognitive processing needed
  • Micro-Animations: Subtle animations celebrate wins and cushion losses, creating emotional engagement
  • Large Typography: Key numbers (prices, changes) are oversized for quick scanning in any lighting
  • Gesture-First: Swipe to trade, pull to refresh, long-press for details – minimizing button taps

Reflection

Key Learnings

Designing for real-time, high-stakes interactions taught me valuable lessons about performance, psychology, and restraint.

  • Less is More (Really): Every piece of data we removed improved engagement. Users don't want all information – they want the right information.
  • Test in Context: Lab testing didn't reveal the chaos of live game usage. Contextual testing during actual games was essential.
  • Emotion Drives Action: The winning trades needed celebration; losses needed soft landings. Emotional design increased repeat engagement.
  • Performance is UX: A 100ms delay during live trading felt like an eternity. Technical performance is a design requirement.
